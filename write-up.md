Why you chose these specific questions

The questions in this reflection tree were designed based on my previous interview experience, where I observed that candidates often provide ideal or socially acceptable answers rather than reflecting their actual behavior. In many interviews, direct questions such as “Are you responsible?” or “Are you a team player?” tend to produce predictable responses. Most candidates already know what the “correct” answer should be, so their responses reflect intention rather than reality. As a result, these types of questions fail to differentiate individuals or reveal meaningful insights into their thinking patterns.

To address this limitation, I designed the questions to focus on real situations and behavioral responses instead of direct self-evaluation. Each question is framed around a specific context, such as when something goes well, when something fails, or when interacting with others. This approach encourages users to recall actual experiences rather than construct ideal answers. For example, asking “What contributed most to the outcome?” indirectly reveals whether a person attributes success to their own effort, external factors, or team support. This method is more effective than directly asking about responsibility because it captures natural attribution patterns.

Additionally, the options for each question were carefully designed to represent realistic behaviors observed in practical settings, including interview scenarios. Instead of presenting binary or leading options, the choices reflect a spectrum of responses that individuals commonly exhibit. This reduces bias and encourages honest selection. For instance, options such as “help proactively,” “help if asked,” and “ignore” capture different levels of contribution without labeling any option as explicitly right or wrong. Overall, the questions were chosen to reveal underlying decision-making patterns through behavior, rather than relying on self-perception or idealized responses.

How you designed the branching — what trade-offs you made

The branching structure of the reflection tree follows a consistent and intentional flow: question, decision, reflection, and transition. Question nodes are used to gather input from the user, while decision nodes interpret those inputs by grouping them into meaningful categories. Reflection nodes provide insights based on the selected path, and bridge nodes ensure a smooth transition between different conceptual stages of the tree. This structure allows the interaction to feel like a guided conversation rather than a static questionnaire.

The tree is organized across three sequential axes: locus of control (ownership), orientation (contribution), and radius (impact). The progression is deliberate. The first axis focuses on how individuals interpret outcomes—whether they attribute results to their own actions or to external circumstances. Once this awareness is established, the second axis shifts attention to how individuals engage with others, particularly whether they contribute to a team or remain self-focused. Finally, the third axis expands the scope to long-term thinking and broader impact, encouraging users to reflect beyond immediate outcomes.

One of the main trade-offs in the design was between depth and simplicity. A highly detailed tree with extensive branching could capture more nuanced behaviors and edge cases, but it would also increase complexity, making the system harder to implement and the interaction harder to follow. I chose a moderate level of branching that balances clarity with meaningful differentiation. Another trade-off was between flexibility and determinism. Since the system uses fixed options and predefined paths, it ensures consistent and reproducible outputs, which is essential for implementation without runtime AI. However, this also limits the ability to capture highly personalized or unexpected responses. To mitigate this, the options were designed carefully to cover a wide range of realistic behaviors while still maintaining structure.

What psychological sources informed your design

The design of the reflection tree is grounded in established psychological theories that explain how individuals perceive control, interact with others, and expand their perspective over time. The first axis is based on the concept of locus of control introduced by Julian Rotter. This theory distinguishes between internal and external attribution, helping identify whether individuals believe they have control over their outcomes or attribute them to external factors such as luck or environment. This forms the foundation for assessing ownership and responsibility.

The second axis is influenced by the work of Carol Dweck, particularly her emphasis on effort, learning, and adaptability. This perspective informs how individuals approach collaboration and improvement. Those with a growth-oriented mindset are more likely to contribute, seek feedback, and engage with others constructively, while others may remain self-focused or avoid collaborative effort.

The third axis draws from the concept of self-transcendence proposed by Abraham Maslow. This theory extends beyond basic needs and self-actualization, focusing on how individuals consider the broader impact of their actions. It supports the transition from immediate, self-centered thinking to long-term, purpose-driven decision-making that considers teams, organizations, and even wider communities.

These psychological foundations are embedded within the structure of the tree rather than being explicitly presented, allowing the system to infer meaningful behavioral patterns while maintaining a simple and user-friendly interaction.

What you'd improve with more time

With more time, the first improvement would be to introduce option-level signaling instead of assigning signals at the node level. Currently, all options within a question contribute equally, but in reality, different responses represent different strengths of behavior. For example, attributing success to personal effort should carry more weight than attributing it to luck, and proactively helping others should be distinguished from helping only when asked. Incorporating weighted signals at the option level would significantly improve the accuracy of the reflection output.

Another important improvement would be enhancing the summary generation. The current system identifies dominant tendencies across the three axes but does not generate deeply personalized insights. A more advanced approach would analyze combinations of responses across multiple nodes and produce tailored reflections that highlight specific behavioral patterns, strengths, and areas for improvement.

Additionally, increasing the depth of branching would allow the system to capture more nuanced behaviors and edge cases. While the current structure maintains clarity and usability, deeper branching could better represent complex real-world scenarios and provide more precise reflections. Along with this, introducing consistency checks would improve the quality of insights. For example, the system could detect contradictions such as a user indicating strong ownership in one part of the tree while attributing outcomes to external factors elsewhere. Identifying such inconsistencies would make the reflection more insightful and meaningful.

Finally, while the current design is fully deterministic and structured for implementation, future iterations could explore hybrid approaches that retain determinism while improving adaptability and personalization. Overall, these improvements would enhance both the depth and accuracy of the reflection process while maintaining the core structure of the system.
